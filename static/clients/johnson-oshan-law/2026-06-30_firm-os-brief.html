Where we'd start

Five candidate builds, ranked by ROI

These are hypotheses from a first look at a corporate boutique like yours. We cut to two or three on the call once we know where your hours actually go. The tag shows which lever each one pulls hardest.

1Time capture + billing narrativesRevenue first

Work product and calendar or DMS activity become draft time entries with clean, client-ready narratives. An attorney edits and approves before anything bills. No legal judgment touched, and recaptured time is usually the single biggest ROI line in a billable boutique.

2Contract review + playbook redliningCost + throughput

Inbound MSAs, NDAs, and DPAs get reviewed against a firm negotiation playbook. The OS returns a redline plus an issues list ranked by risk; an attorney approves. High-volume, repeatable associate hours come back.

3Financing + commercial draftingCost + speed

Draft SAFEs, notes, priced-round docs, and standard agreements off your own precedent, then redline the counterparty markup and flag off-market terms. Core practice, heavy per-matter hours, faster turnaround.

4Diligence + data-room triageCost + risk-catch

Extract the terms that matter (change-of-control, assignment, MFN, exclusivity), build the diligence checklist status, and draft disclosure schedules. The single biggest grind when M&A or a financing hits, with an attorney validating every finding.

5Entity-maintenance agentRecurring revenue

A scheduled agent that watches annual reports, franchise tax, and minute-book upkeep across the client portfolio, drafts the filing, and flags what's due. A self-running agent is the real differentiator, and this one is a productizable recurring-revenue offering and a retention hook.

The enabling layer: a precedent and clause library mined from your closed matters. It's what the drafting and redlining agents run on, and often the first foundation worth building. On the call we'll learn whether that library exists as a firm asset or lives in senior lawyers' heads.
