SSA-3373 · Section C

Physical abilities

Social Security compares these numbers against your doctors' records. Answers that sound more capable than your medical file can quietly hurt your claim — the guide flags those as you type.

The heaviest you can lift and carry, even occasionally.

Worth a second look Applicants with back or orthopedic conditions usually report lifting 10–15 lbs at most. A higher number than your records support can make a reviewer question the whole report. Lift what's true on a bad day, not your best day.
Worth a second look Standing more than ~2 hours straight reads as fairly capable. If your condition forces you to shift, sit, or rest, describe the typical limit — not the rare good stretch.
Worth a second look Sitting comfortably for more than ~2 hours can undercut a claim built on chronic pain. If you need to get up and move, that detail matters — say so.
SSA-3373 · Section B

A typical day

This is the most-read part of the form. Describing a fully independent day is the most common mistake — it tells the reviewer you can function. Describe a realistic day, including the hard parts.

Worth a second look You've marked yourself independent on most daily activities. That's the single most common way a strong claim gets weakened — it reads as "able to function." If any of these are only possible on good days, take longer than they used to, or need breaks or help, the form has room to say that. Be accurate, not modest.

SSA-3373 · Section D

Treatment

Consistent treatment is one of the strongest credibility signals there is. Gaps in care are the first thing a reviewer points to.

Worth a second look A reviewer reads little or no current treatment as "the condition can't be that limiting." If cost, transportation, or access is the reason you're not in care, that explanation belongs on the form — it changes how the gap is read.
SSA-3368 · Work

Are you working now?

This is the first thing Social Security checks. Earnings above the "substantial gainful activity" limit can end a claim at Step 1 — before anyone even looks at your medical condition.

Important — Step 1 risk Earnings at or above the monthly "substantial gainful activity" limit (about $1,550) usually mean an automatic technical denial, no matter how serious your condition is. If your work is part-time, supported, or you've had to cut back, those facts matter and belong on the form.
SSA-3368 · Medical

Your conditions & doctors

List every condition and every provider. A condition with no supporting doctor is hard for Social Security to credit.

SSA-3369 · Past work

Your most recent job

How physically demanding your past work was decides which jobs Social Security expects you could still do. Understating the demands of old jobs can work against you, especially if you're 50 or older.

Good to capture Heavier past work, described accurately, can actually help — under Social Security's "grid rules," someone 50+ who can no longer do physical work they used to do may qualify more easily. Don't downplay how demanding the job really was.
Worth a second look The weight you entered is heavier than the exertion level you picked above. Social Security cross-checks these — make sure the job's category matches what you actually lifted, or the report looks inconsistent.
