§1

What we found

Two issues with the setup today. The brief skill leaves load-bearing rules to instruction where they need to be deterministic. And the workflow around the brief is manual end-to-end — even when the brief itself lands clean.

Issue 1 of 2Brief writing: instruction where determinism is needed

The existing brief skill has the knowledge — your style guide, your regulatory reference, your 14-item Special Issues Checklist, the legal-substance bugs you flagged in Session 1 are all in there. But it enforces a number of load-bearing rules through instructions Claude can decline to follow on a given case.

Three categories of error surfaced

The Nylander brief from 4/25 is the most-instrumented evidence we have. At first glance it reads well; under inspection, three categories of error showed up. None are Nylander-specific.

1
Citation errors — the cite either doesn't exist where the skill claims, isn't pinpoint enough to be usable, or follows the wrong format entirely:
  • Unsupported. Ex. 9F cited for hip pain; 9F doesn't document persistent post-surgical hip pain. The actual support is at 6F/1 and 16F/2.
  • Bare-exhibit to multi-page exhibits — not usable when 12F runs 214 pages and 13F runs 287.
  • Wrong format across tools. The medical-summary tool you've been using emits Bates / absolute-PDF citations on the Bethea record instead of (XF/N) — exactly the "the cite shape is wrong, so even correct cites are unusable" problem you flagged in Session 1. Different tool, different claimant, same defect.
2
Factual errors — the brief takes a position on a fact in the record without grounding it in extracted source. The Nylander instance: the skill agreed with the state agency's PRW finding at Step 4; the agency had actually concluded the opposite (2A/7 and 4A/7). The Step 4 argument is fundamentally different in each posture. The shape applies anywhere prose is asked to recall what the agency said before agreeing or disagreeing — SGA at Step 1, RFC findings, Step 5 jobs cited, education classification.
3
Contextual errors — a term in the record carries local meaning that drops out on the way to drafting. The Nylander instance: "OCD" in the record was Osteochondritis Dissecans; the skill categorized Mr. Nylander as Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder and built a CPP paragraph around it. Broader than abbreviations: any term where source-page context is what disambiguates the meaning. Medical abbreviations are the highest-leverage instance — OCD, MS, CP, DJD, DDD all have multiple expansions — but they're not the only one.

The fix is mechanism, not more instruction

Your 4/27 changelog is the right move under the constraint of pure prose — new "Accuracy Discipline" section, fact-verification-protocol.md, expanded Common Errors list, page-pinpoint format table. The instinct is correct: each of these failure modes is recurring, and they need to be engineered against, not just patched per case.

But the medium is the limit. Adding more prose pushes the median case in the right direction; it doesn't make the marginal case deterministic — instructions Claude reads on each case will keep being optional in the cases at the edges. The fix is moving the load-bearing rules off prose entirely, onto a precomputed structured case-state — extracted facts, indexed abbreviations, mechanically-resolved citations — that the brief drafts against rather than recalls. §2's Module 1 is that case-state, plus a verification pass over the draft before it leaves the skill.

Issue 2 of 2Workflow: manual end-to-end

Even when a brief lands clean, the loop around it is manual today — staff downloads from Assure, you spin up the brief tool, load the PDF, type the prompt, wait, copy back, edit, file. Each step is a potential dropped ball; each step is friction that doesn't compound away no matter how good the brief gets.

§2 closes that loop too — and we run it for you. Your staff drops the PDF in one place; the brief, worksheet, and chronology appear in another; you get an email when they're ready. The remaining touch points collapse to: review and edit (yours), file (yours). Everything between those two endpoints runs as a managed service we operate on your behalf.

Three layers we add

Layer 1 · Exhibit-aware ingestion. The Assure PDF is parsed once into structured chunks — exhibit-letter + internal-page, the (XF/N) reference shape end-to-end. Built on top of the extract_targeted.py infrastructure already in your existing skill bundle. Drafting, citation verification, and every downstream check runs against this structure, not raw PDF text.

If a value is in case_state.json, the drafting step reads it; it doesn't re-derive it. Same pattern as moving citation verification from instruction to mechanical check — produces consistent results because there's nothing for Claude to inconsistently decide.

The four categories of values:

Category A Date and age math Pure arithmetic on extracted dates. Highest leverage: gets the grid analysis right.
  • age_at_AOD, age_at_hearing — DOB + onset / DOB + hearing date
  • age_category_at_AOD, age_category_at_hearing — lookup against the four SSA grid age categories (<50, 50–54, 55–59, 60+)
  • crosses_age_boundary — claimant's category at hearing exceeds category at AOD; triggers bifurcated argument structure
  • borderline_at_AOD, borderline_at_hearing — within 6 months of any age boundary; triggers borderline-age argument per POMS HA 01220.042
  • obesity_classification — BMI math from documented height/weight, mapped to Class I (30.0–34.9), II (35.0–39.9), III (≥40.0)
Category B Verbatim agency findings — extracted from the DDE
Principle: when the agency made a finding, the skill extracts the verbatim language before drafting any agree-or-disagree. Hearing-stage cases by definition went through both an initial determination (1A or 2A) and a reconsideration determination (3A or 4A) — that's the prerequisite that gets the case to a hearing — so both DDEs are present in the record. Your 4/27 Nylander changelog already encodes this rule for the PRW finding at /7; the update generalizes it to the other load-bearing fields. If a field isn't populated for a particular case, the skill flags it in the verification report rather than inventing a value.
  • DDE_SGA_finding — verbatim conclusion at /1
  • DDE_PRW_finding — verbatim conclusion at /7 of both DDEs
  • DDE_RFC_finding — what RFC the agency assessed (sedentary / light / medium / heavy + non-exertional limitations); determines the 20x.xx grid table
  • DDE_jobs_cited_at_step5 — DOT codes + titles cited (when denial reached Step 5); feeds the obsolete-jobs check
  • claimant_education_level — illiterate / marginal / limited / high school / college, per DDE classification (grid input)
  • prw_lookback_window — DLI + hearing date with explicit DLI-pivot
  • prw_jobs, prw_within_lookback — for each PRW job: title/employer/dates/DOT/SVP plus boolean against the lookback window. Sources: 21E and 3E
  • dli_in_past / dli_in_future, title_of_claim, onset_amended_flag, hearing_modality
Category C Reference-table lookups Static reference data the skill can't reliably recall accurately from training. Ships as data files alongside the skill.
  • SGA_threshold_by_year — annual SGA values 1990–current. Today the skill says "$1,090/mo. in 2015, rising — check current threshold"; Claude doesn't reliably know current values
  • applicable_grid_rule — given age category + education + RFC + transferability finding, the rule number (e.g., 201.06, 202.06) is a deterministic lookup against the six tables in your regulatory-quick-reference.md. Today derived in prose
  • obsolete_jobs_match — cross-reference DDS-cited jobs against the obsolete list (addresser, nut sorter, lens inserter, preparer, charge-account clerk, telegraph operator)
  • transferability_test_required — claimant 55+ limited to sedentary OR 60+ limited to light → POMS DI 25015.017 "very little, if any, vocational adjustment" standard applies
Category D Cross-references against extracted infrastructure
  • exhibit_size_map — already produced by extract_targeted.py in meta.json. Plumbs into citation verification: any bare-exhibit citation auto-fails unless the exhibit's page count is 1
  • abbreviation_map — every medical abbreviation in F-series records paired with its source-page spelling-out. When ambiguous (OCD, MS, CP, DJD, DDD), the disambiguation is the verbatim spelling-out from the record. Closes the OCD failure mode

The ingestion helper

A small standalone skill (wiki-ingest) you invoke conversationally: "ingest this NOSSCR bulletin into the wiki" — drop a URL, PDF, or transcript, and a properly-structured .md draft appears in <wiki-root>/_drafts/ for you to review. The helper:

  • Decides whether the content is a new article or an addition to an existing one
  • Drafts following the convention your existing 26 articles already use (# Title H1, *Source basis:* … line, prose body with H2/H3, ## Related Articles cross-links)
  • Proposes related-article cross-links and updates the index when you accept
  • Files the source PDF (if applicable) so the source-basis line can pinpoint-cite a stable filename

You move the draft from _drafts/ into the live wiki when satisfied. The move is the publish action. The skill only reads from the live folder, so a draft article cannot accidentally land in a brief.

What changes for you

  • The wiki stops being a black box.
  • NOSSCR bulletins, judge decisions, CLE materials, blog posts flow in with one prompt.
  • The brief skill gets smarter every time you add an article — case-profile loader picks up new articles based on their frontmatter triggers. No central manifest to maintain.

What stays the same

  • The 26 existing articles.
  • The 9-section structure of your index.md.
  • The Karpathy article shape.
  • You as the editor — accept, edit, or reject every draft. Direct hand-editing is supported; the helper is the expected path, not the only one.
