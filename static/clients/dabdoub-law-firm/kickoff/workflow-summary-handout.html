The Finished Picture

What Your Day Looks Like When This Is Done

This is the finished picture: your appeals work once the build is complete. Same team, same expertise, far less busywork.

The retyping, the chasing, the digging through a file for a date, the blank page at the start of every letter: that is the part we are taking off your plate. The judgment, the client relationships, the call on whether anything is right: that stays yours.

Read it as a destination. Each stage below shows your work today next to your work when we're done, with a checklist of exactly what changes. Mark it up. Tell us where it would actually help and where it would just get in the way.

1
Stage 1
A case opens
Today

Intake marks the case "Closed Won," it gets assigned, and you set up the new case by hand: the folder, the intro email, the first round of tasks.

When we're done

The moment a case opens, the new-case intro email is drafted for you and the case folder is created and organized automatically. You start from a set-up case, not a blank one.

What comes off your plate
  • The new-case intro email writes its own first draft.
  • The case folder is created from the standard template, automatically.
  • The opening tasks are already assigned and waiting.
2
Stage 2
The appeal deadline gets set
Today

You read the denial letter, find the appeal deadline, and calendar it with 2-week, 1-week, and day-of reminders across the right calendars, by hand, every time.

When we're done

The denial is read the moment it arrives, the deadline and the key facts surface for you to confirm, and once you confirm, the full reminder cadence fires on its own. You confirm; the system does the rest.

What comes off your plate
  • The denial letter is read on arrival and the deadline is proposed for you.
  • The 2-week, 1-week, and day-of reminders fire automatically once you confirm the date.
  • Every reminder leads with the client name and case ID, so you always know which file it means.

You still own the calendar.

The system proposes the date; a person always reads the letter and confirms it. That confirmation is never automated.

3
Stage 3
The file gets organized
Today

You sort each file into the right folders by hand, and the firm is mid-move from the S drive to new storage, so where things live keeps shifting.

When we're done

The matter folder is built from the standard template the moment a case opens, and everything the AI produces lands in a clearly labeled "AI output" subfolder inside it, with a filename that identifies the case, no matter where the storage lives.

What comes off your plate
  • The matter folder and subfolders are created from the template, automatically.
  • Every AI draft lands in the same predictable place, never somewhere strange.
  • You get an alert (Teams or email) with the file and a link you can open on your phone.
4
Stage 4
Claim file, policy, and homework
Today

This is where a lot of your day goes: ordering the claim file and policy, sending the treating-doctor form, setting up the homework call, then tracking IME / FCE / CPET scheduling, reminding the attorney to prep the cover letter, reminding the client, and chasing reports. All of it typed and tracked by hand.

When we're done

The request letters and emails draft themselves, the scheduling and reminders run on a cadence, and the testing cover letter and records packet are prepared for you a week before each evaluation. You are reviewing and sending, not typing from scratch and remembering every follow-up.

What comes off your plate
  • The claim file request and the policy request draft themselves.
  • The treating-doctor info request and the homework-call setup emails draft themselves.
  • Follow-ups on records requests fire on schedule.
  • IME / FCE / CPET scheduling is coordinated, with 2-week, 1-week, and day-before reminders.
  • The testing cover letter and the medical-records packet are prepped a week before each evaluation.
  • Report follow-ups with providers are tracked for you.
5
Stage 5
You mark "Homework Complete"
Today

You check the "Homework Complete" box, which tells the attorney the appeal is ready to write. The attorney then opens a cold stack of documents.

When we're done

You check the same box. The instant you do, the whole claim file is read and turned into a structured summary: key dates, policy provisions, the carrier's denial reasoning, the treating physicians' statements, and the IME conclusions. The attorney starts from an organized summary instead of a cold file.

What comes off your plate
  • Nothing new for you to do; you check the same box you check today.
  • The file-digging that used to slow down the next step is just done.
  • The attorney opens an organized summary, not a stack of PDFs.
6
Stage 6
The appeal letter gets written and sent
Today

The attorney drafts the appeal from a blank template. You assemble the submission packet by hand (letter, evidence, Request for Information, Direction of Payment, Authorization), fax and email or mail it, set the acknowledgement follow-up, and send the client a copy.

When we're done

A first-draft appeal letter writes itself in the firm's own voice, pulling from the claim-file summary, the denial, the policy, and the firm's own past appeals, carrier-aware and condition-aware. It lands in the case folder with an alert. The submission packet is bundled for you, the acknowledgement chase fires on its own, and the client copy goes out automatically.

What comes off your plate
  • The first-draft appeal letter writes itself, in the firm's voice, from the firm's own past work.
  • Every draft shows the past appeals and templates it pulled from, so you can see where it came from.
  • The submission packet is assembled for you.
  • The 1-week carrier acknowledgement chase fires automatically.
  • The client copy of the appeal is sent automatically.
  • SSDI-attorney evidence forwards itself.

It is always a first draft.

The attorney still owns the letter. The win is that a solid draft is waiting instead of a blank page.

7
Stage 7
Decision deadlines and medical-review rebuttals
Today

You calendar the 45-day and 90-day decision deadlines by hand. When a medical review comes in, the attorney decides the response, you calendar the deadline, and you draft and submit the rebuttal from scratch.

When we're done

The 45-day and 90-day deadlines calendar themselves from the submission date. Incoming medical reviews are sorted and their response deadlines set for you. And a first-draft rebuttal, in the firm's voice from its own past responses, is waiting when you need it.

What comes off your plate
  • The 45-day and 90-day decision deadlines are calendared automatically from the submission date.
  • Incoming medical reviews are sorted and their response deadlines calendared.
  • A first-draft medical-review rebuttal writes itself, the same way the appeal letter does.
  • The 45-day enforcement letter drafts itself when a deadline is missed, and extensions get calendared.
8
Stage 8
Approval or denial
Today

When a case resolves, you log the outcome and then work the follow-ups by hand: the next-steps email to the client, the termination letters, the routing, the marketing hand-off.

When we're done

The routing already happens automatically, and now the letters that hang off each outcome draft themselves too, the same way the appeal letter does. You log the outcome; the follow-up drafts arrive ready for review.

What comes off your plate
  • The "next steps" email to the client after an approval drafts itself.
  • Termination letters and second-appeal restarts draft themselves.
  • The hand-off to On Claims and the marketing routing happen automatically.

A note on the client portal

When this is done, clients can upload their homework and documents and see their case status themselves, through a portal. That means fewer "did you get my form?" calls landing on you, and less manual follow-up to track down what a client still owes.
